1. What is a Ledger?

In the simplest terms, a ledger is a "Book of Final Entry." While a journal records every transaction as it happens (chronologically), the **ledger** organizes those transactions by account. If you want to know how much cash you have, you look at the Cash Ledger. If you want to know how much a specific customer owes you, you look at their Individual Ledger.

In 2026, ledger accounting remains the core of financial reporting. Whether it's a paper book or a digital app like KhataSetu, the logic remains the same: it is the source of truth for your business's financial health.

2. The Golden Rules: Debits and Credits

Most beginners find debits and credits confusing because they think in "bank statement" terms. In accounting, these simply mean "Left" and "Right."

The Accounting Equation:

Assets = Liabilities + Equity

For every transaction, this equation must remain balanced. This is why it is called "Double-Entry" accounting.

  • Debits (Dr): Record an increase in Assets or Expenses.
  • Credits (Cr): Record an increase in Liabilities, Income, or Equity.

3. Understanding the T-Account

The T-account is a visual representation of a ledger account. It looks like the letter "T," separating debits from credits.

By using this method, a business owner can calculate the "Balance" of an account at any time by subtracting the total of one side from the other. Manual T-accounts are slow, which is why digital ledgers have become the standard for modern shops.

4. The Accounting Cycle in 2026

Accounting is a repeating cycle. It begins when a customer walks into your shop and ends with a financial report. The steps include:

  1. Identifying the Transaction: (Example: Selling goods for $100).
  2. Journalizing: Recording the raw data.
  3. Posting to the Ledger: Moving that data into the specific customer's or category's account.
  4. Adjusting Entries: Accounting for things like depreciation or unpaid bills.
  5. Financial Statements: Generating a Profit & Loss report.

6. How Ledgers Drive Profitability

A ledger isn't just for tax compliance; it is a management tool. By reviewing your ledger, you can see which customers are late on payments and which products are generating the most cash. Good ledger management allows you to make data-driven decisions rather than guessing your bank balance.
